To what extent does the narrator’s description of scents in Patrick Suskind’s Perfume help the reader experience synesthesia, validating Ramachandran’s theories regarding synesthesia?

  • 1a. Cite thorough textual evidence to support analysis of what the text says

Perfume: The Story of a Murder is 1985 a historical fantasy novel by German writer and screenwriter Patrick Suskind. The story takes place in eighteen century France and follows the life of Jean-Baptise Grenouille, who possesses extraordinary olfaction. As Grenouille matures, he finds himself desperately attracted to the odor of pubescent girls. This sends him on a killing streak in which he murders 25 young women, intending to develop a perfume made of their scents. Ironically, however, the perfume he so painstakingly creates eventually becomes the cause of his own death. While the novel's concentration is on scent more than anything else, it mainly relies on extremely graphic descriptions of scent. This mix creates a horrific world that is both revolting and beautiful, drawing the reader in while yet pushing them away. To allow the readers to understand the mind of Grenouille through words, Suskind used a variety of strategies such as diction, juxtaposition, and much more, but most importantly the utilization of synesthesia.

Synesthesia — derived from the Greek meaning, “to perceive together” — is a neurological condition in which one sensory experience, such as hearing, involuntarily activates another sense, such as taste, simultaneously. Considering that synesthetes can experience any combination of the senses, there are a considerable number of synesthesia subtypes (“Synesthesia”).

What are some of the more common types of synesthesia? Some of the more common types of synesthesia are:

  • Auditory-tactile synesthesia: a sound triggers a bodily sensation
  • Chromesthesia: a sound prompts the sight of a color
  • Grapheme-color synesthesia: letters and numbers are associated with colors
  • Lexical-gustatory synesthesia: a word triggers a taste

Synesthesia is quite subjective; even if two individuals had the same type of synesthesia, they would have different triggers and effects. For example, in grapheme-color synesthetes, one person might associate the word “chair” with the color blue while another may associate the word “tree” with the color red.

Surprisingly, it isn’t rare for people to fake synesthesia for attention, which is why a few scientists conducted an experiment to explain/prove the distinction between synesthetes and non-synesthetes. So, how can we determine if a person has synesthesia? When synesthetes are asked to select what they experience or see by hearing or looking at certain things, 90 percent of them tend to select the same exact thing whereas only 30-40 percent of non-synesthesia choose the same exact thing as others. A different way, a more precise way, of proving synesthesia is by using the DTI, diffusion tensor imaging, which is a brain imaging technology. This technology traces the water flow in the brain, which helps the scientists to find the number of axons or the "white matter" to see which regions of the brain are connected (Gaidos, 2009). This technology could also be used to see which type of synesthesia one experiences.

However, some researchers argued that synesthesia has less to do with sensation and to do more with semantics (Frohlich, 2017). Also known as ideasthesia, people might connect the trigger or the inducer's literal meaning in order to connect it with certain senses. For instance, some synesthetes have the ability to connect months or spaces with geometrical shapes which could prove the theory of ideasthesia that their understanding of the literal/semantic – could be the number of months or estimated area of that space – could lead them to link it with a geometrical shape with that specific amount of edges or corners.

Along with many other scientists, V.S. Ramachandran, an Indian-American neuroscientist, well known for creating the mirror box treatment, made a contribution to the study of synesthesia. According to his theory, synesthesia is caused by the cross-wiring in our brains which are "excessive neural connections'' between functional areas, and it can cause one of the cortical areas – part of the brain where thoughts, emotion, memory, and more processes – to trigger another cortical area.

Why would that happen? Well, he suggested that everyone, even non-synesthetes, have each region of their brain connected to other parts but they are all trimmed by their genes to form the "characteristic modular architecture" of the brain function we are used to. However, in the synesthete's brain, a heretical gene that is mutated lacks the ability to “trim” those connections between different parts of the brain which leaves them with synesthesia(being able to connect different sensations). Interestingly, this is why synesthesia is eight times more common between artists and novelists, which is also why they are able to create a connection between seemingly unrelated things.

In Perfume, Grenouille exhibits a remarkable sense of smell. To enhance the reading experience, the author uses vivid imagery, possibly allowing the readers to get a glimpse of Grenouille’s world of smells.

Brain scans have shown that reading comprehension descriptions “stimulate the brain and even [impact] how we act in life” (Paul 2012). In 2006, a study was directed by researchers in Spain in which participants were asked to read both scent-associated and neutral words while their brains were scanned by fMRI (functional magnetic resonance imaging). It was revealed that the primary olfactory cortex glowed when scent-associated words were read, and remained dark when neutral words were read. Furthermore, in 2012, researchers from Emory University conducted a study and came across similar results. They discovered that the sensory cortex became active when participants read metaphors involving textures, for instance, “She had glossy eyes”.

Through these experiments, it can be concluded that the brain does not entirely distinguish between reading about an event and actually experiencing it. In both cases, regions of the brain are activated. As Perfume heavily focuses on the senses, readers may be able to feel as if they were in the story.

Throughout Perfume, there are a number of cases in which the narrator uses vivid diction to associate odors with other senses and emotions, ultimately allowing the readers to experience a variety of synesthesia subtypes to some extent.

One type of synesthesia that the readers may encounter is olfactory-visual synesthesia, in which odors are perceived as colors, shapes, etcetera. In chapter 7 of the book, the smell of the sea is referred to as “a sail whose billows had caught up water, salt, and a cold sun” (Suskind 37). The image of a ship on water is associated with this smell. A similar instance occurs in chapter 35, where the scent of a red-headed girl is “seen” as a picture of her “jumping about, warming up and then cooling off, apparently playing some game” (Suskind 176). The aforementioned examples enable the reader to associate an image with a scent, which is essentially synesthesia. In chapter 39, the odor given off by an adolescent female “shimmered with a thousand colors” (Suskind 196). Furthermore, in chapter 12, the scent of ‘Amor and Psyche’, a perfume by Pelissier, is said to possess a “rich brown depth”. These examples correlate odors with colors, again allowing the reader to experience one sense through another.

Other synesthesia subtypes the readers could experience include personifying and emotional synesthesia. In chapter 12, Baldini calls Pelissier’s perfume “cheerful” and “charming”, and claims that it “puts you in a good mood at once” (Suskind 64). Baldini first associates the perfume’s notes with a bright and bubbly personality, then with feelings of happiness. In the second paragraph of page 128, the scent of Grenouille’s mother is described as “homodical”. Here, an odor is also equated with a personality. Furthermore, in chapter 27, “the odor of [a particular] morning was for Grenouille the odor of hope” (Suskind 134). This is yet another association between a scent and a feeling.

Olfactory-tactile synesthesia, in which tactile sensations are triggered by specific smells, is yet another type of synesthesia the book exemplifies (“Olfactory-tactile synesthesia”). In the second paragraph of page 41, the narrator describes an unnamed odor as “warm”. Here, the sensation of being warm is associated with a scent.

According to Ramachandran, even though synesthesia is about eight times more common among artists, poets, and other creative workers, it is visible to everyone to a certain degree. How do we understand and validate his theory via the book Perfume? When you are reading keywords describing scent, i.e. “rich brown depth” when describing a perfume in chapter 12, your brain visualizes or creates an illusion of you experiencing the scent to help you understand the seemingly plain and senseless words better. This is essentially a validation of his theory since the visual and odor senses were supposedly “trimmed”, but when reading our brain automatically does a cross model synesthetic abstraction and “connects” the two different senses. Thus, creating an illusion of perceiving such scent.

In chapter 49, the book ushered its finale, the most spectacular orgy in human history where tens of thousands of people engage in sexual intercourse with no regard for who their partner was. Instantaneously after capturing the odor of Grenouille’s perfume, people who were furious with the notorious serial killer suddenly deify Grenouille, with only a simple drop of the perfume. Even though it may be foolish to attempt to uncover the science behind a fictional story, modern science related to synesthesia can possibly explain the peculiar behavior of the characters involved in the orgy.

In the story, Grenouille coveted love and soul due to the fact that he is unable to scent himself, and to a person who identifies with scent, it is no different than being denied of existence in his world, and the perfume itself is how Grenouille portrays love through olfaction. Combined with our knowledge of synesthesia, even though we can’t be certain what the characters experience, we can conduct an educated guess of the explanation of the characters’ behavior. Since the perfume represents love, the characters experienced their very-own utopia in their mind, the orgy was solely to express the satisfaction of the experience and not due to being sexually aroused. Laure Richis marries his beloved and admired daughter in his mind; the bishop was inspired to religious rapture for the first time; each and every one of them experienced the love they coveted, just as how Grenouille designed the perfume. They all have experienced synesthesia to the most extreme level, when the olfactory “connects” with every other sensory to a degree where it has replaced the consciousness of the character. It created an illusion for the characters of being in a utopia, allowing them to display absurd behavior in a normal person’s eyes.

To give credit to the author of Perfume, Suskind, the excessive and precise use of graphical sensory descriptions is perhaps the reason Perfume was an immense success, such words along with metalepsis allow the reader to immerse themselves into the mind of Grenouille and develop our very own synesthetic experience.
